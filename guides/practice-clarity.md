# Practice Clarity

## The Question Nobody Prepares You For

> **Foundation Guide**
>
> Everything else in the Alexander Watson library builds on the ideas introduced here.
>
> If you're new to the library, start here. Before websites, before copywriting and before SEO comes something more fundamental: understanding the practice you're trying to describe.

"Tell me what you do."

It's usually the first question I ask when I meet a therapist.

Most people laugh.

It's a strange question, coming from the person who's supposed to be talking about websites.

Then they pause.

Not because they don't know.

They've been doing the work for years, sometimes decades.

The pause isn't confusion.

It's something closer to a search, for an answer to a question they've never actually had to give before.

Not outside a therapy room.

Not to someone who wasn't already a client.

I've seen this pause in therapists three months into practice.

I've seen it in therapists with twenty years behind them.

I've seen it in therapists other therapists refer their most difficult cases to.

Being good at the work, it turns out, has almost no relationship to being able to describe it.

At first I assumed this was a writing problem.

Some people are naturally more comfortable with words than others, and therapists, whatever else they are, aren't always writers.

So for a while, my job looked like helping people find better words.

It didn't work.

Not reliably.

I'd help someone write a clear, warm, specific paragraph about their practice.

A few weeks later, they'd quietly rewrite it back into something vaguer.

Not because the clearer version was wrong.

Because it made them uncomfortable in a way they couldn't quite explain.

And vagueness, whatever its costs, rarely makes anyone uncomfortable.

That's when I started paying closer attention to where the discomfort actually came from.

## It wasn't the writing

Here's what I noticed, slowly, across enough conversations that it stopped looking like coincidence.

The therapists who struggled most to describe their work weren't struggling to find the right words.

They were struggling with something underneath the words.

An unresolved question about what, specifically, they believed they offered...

...and to whom.

The writing wasn't the problem.

It was simply the first place the problem became visible.

Writing demands an answer be committed to.

In public.

Without the option of qualifying it further.

Therapy doesn't.

Every session adapts.

Every client is different.

A website cannot.

It has to say something, once, to a stranger, before any relationship exists to soften it.

That's exactly the condition under which an unresolved question about your own work stops being harmless...

...and starts becoming visible.

Therapy training teaches you how to understand other people.

It rarely asks you to define yourself.

I began thinking of this as **Definer's Block**.

<div class="acw-callout acw-framework">

<strong>Definer's Block</strong>

The inability to put into words something you understand instinctively but have never consciously defined.

</div>

It rarely has anything to do with vocabulary.

It has to do with a question that's simply never been asked directly before.

If you've ever rewritten your homepage half a dozen times...

...or found yourself changing your About page every few months...

...or struggled to answer a simple question about what makes your work different...

...there's a good chance you weren't looking for better words.

You were trying to answer a question you hadn't yet fully answered for yourself.

That's a very different problem.

And it's the reason I no longer begin with copywriting.

I begin with clarity.
---

## The pattern kept repeating

Once I started looking for this, I saw it everywhere.

Not as one obvious problem.

As lots of smaller ones that all turned out to have the same cause.

One therapist described her work like this.

> "I work with adults experiencing anxiety, depression, stress and life transitions."

Twenty minutes into our conversation she described something completely different.

She spoke about the people she loved working with.

People who had spent years being the reliable one.

The person everyone else depended on.

The person who kept everything together.

Until one day they quietly realised they couldn't do it anymore.

That description never appeared on her website.

It felt too specific.

Too narrow.

Almost as though claiming it would somehow exclude everyone else.

Yet it was the only thing she'd said all afternoon that a prospective client could genuinely recognise themselves in.

Another therapist apologised before telling me what he specialised in.

Not afterwards.

Before.

Almost as though naming a focus at all was something he hadn't yet earned the right to do.

Then there was the experienced psychodynamic therapist whose homepage began with her modality.

It was the very first sentence.

The problem wasn't that psychodynamic therapy lacked value.

Far from it.

The problem was that the people she most wanted to help weren't searching for a modality.

They were searching for someone who understood what life currently felt like.

Her first sentence answered a question they hadn't asked yet.

Each conversation looked different.

One felt like confidence.

One felt like training.

One looked like copywriting.

One looked like marketing.

For a long time I treated them as separate problems.

It took longer than I'd like to admit before I realised they all belonged to the same one.

---

## What they actually had in common

Every one of those therapists understood their own work with remarkable fluency.

Ask them in supervision what they believed about change.

Ask them what patterns they noticed across clients.

Ask them what kind of therapeutic relationship they naturally built.

The answers came easily.

Often with extraordinary precision.

Certainly with far more precision than anything written on their websites.

What none of them had ever really been asked to do...

...was close the distance between that private understanding...

...and somebody else's understanding.

Not another therapist.

Not a supervisor.

Not an existing client.

A stranger.

Someone scanning quickly.

Someone already anxious.

Someone unsure whether to keep reading.

Someone whose only access to the therapist was the words on a screen.

That distance is real.

And once you start noticing it...

...you see it everywhere.

It's the distance between what a therapist knows...

...and what another person can recognise.

It's the distance between lived experience...

...and communication.

Most websites don't fail because the therapist lacks insight.

They fail because that insight never quite makes the journey into language.

What's interesting is that you can almost watch the distance shrinking.

When therapists become clearer about what they're really trying to say...

...their enquiries often begin to change.

Not necessarily because more people get in touch.

Because different people do.

People who already recognise themselves.

People who arrive with a much clearer sense of what the therapist actually offers.

That isn't magic.

It isn't persuasion.

It's simply what happens when understanding becomes easier.

And that changed the way I thought about websites forever.

The website wasn't creating the clarity.

It was revealing whether the clarity already existed.

## Where the website actually sits

This was the point where I stopped thinking about websites as the thing I was being asked to fix.

A website isn't where clarity gets created.

By the time anyone reaches the point of writing a homepage...

...the thinking that homepage depends on has usually already happened.

Or it hasn't.

A website cannot answer the question:

**"What do you do?"**

It can only reveal the answer that already exists.

I've never seen that rule broken.

Not once.

I've seen beautiful websites built on unclear thinking.

I've seen modest websites built on extraordinary clarity.

The design changes.

The photography changes.

The platform changes.

The principle never does.

<div class="acw-callout acw-framework">

<strong>THE MIRROR PRINCIPLE</strong>

A website does not create clarity.

It reflects whatever clarity already exists.

</div>

That single idea explains almost every redesign I've ever been asked to rescue.

Someone changes the photographs.

Updates the colours.

Rewrites the homepage.

Moves sections around.

Changes the navigation.

Perhaps even hires another designer.

For a short while the website feels new.

Then, gradually, something starts to feel wrong again.

Not because the design failed.

Because the website was never the thing creating the understanding.

It was only ever reflecting it.

Imagine standing in front of a mirror.

If your shirt is creased, polishing the mirror doesn't help.

If your hair is untidy, replacing the mirror doesn't solve it.

The reflection faithfully shows whatever already exists.

Websites behave in exactly the same way.

A clearer design can make existing clarity easier to see.

It cannot manufacture clarity that isn't already there.

Once you've seen the Mirror Principle, it's remarkably difficult to unsee it.

You begin recognising it everywhere.

You visit therapist websites and immediately notice that something feels unsettled long before you can explain why.

You read a homepage that lists twenty presenting issues but somehow says very little.

You notice websites that feel beautifully designed yet strangely anonymous.

You realise the problem isn't visual.

It isn't technical.

It isn't even the writing.

It's that the thinking underneath the writing hasn't quite settled yet.

That realisation changed how I work.

I stopped asking clients what they wanted their website to say.

I started asking questions about the practice itself.

Who are the people you consistently do your best work with?

What conversations leave you feeling energised?

What do clients thank you for that you never advertise?

What have you quietly come to believe about change after years of sitting with people?

Those answers almost never appear immediately.

But when they do...

...the writing becomes surprisingly straightforward.

Because the words were never the difficult part.

They were waiting for something underneath them to become clear.

## Before anything else

Most therapists assume they need a better website.

Some do.

But not first.

First they need a clearer understanding of the practice they're trying to describe.

Everything else becomes easier after that.

That is **Practice Clarity**.

Before we think about websites...

...before SEO...

...before copywriting...

...before branding...

...we have to answer a much quieter question.

**What, exactly, are we trying to describe?**

Everything else in this guide is an attempt to answer that question.

Not your words.
